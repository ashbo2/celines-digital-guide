Module 1

The Building Blocks

Every screen you ever look at — computer, phone, or tablet — is made up of the same handful of pieces. Once you learn to spot them, nothing on any screen will ever feel unfamiliar.

Screens Are "Choose Your Own Adventure" Books

Think back to reading a book. You open it, you see a page with words and pictures, and you read it. A screen is like that — it shows you information. But here's the key difference:

In a book, you just read. On a screen, you can DO things.

You can tap a word and instantly jump to a new page. You can press a button and make something happen. You can type something and get a response. Each screen is like a page in a "choose your own adventure" book — you look at what's there, make a choice, and it takes you somewhere new.

Think of it this way

Imagine you're reading a recipe book, but instead of just reading the recipe, you could tap on any ingredient and it would add it to your shopping list automatically. That's what a screen does — it lets you interact with the information, not just look at it.

This is true for every single screen you'll ever see — on your computer, on your iPhone, on your iPad, on someone else's device. They're all interactive pages. And just like every recipe has the same basic ingredients (a title, ingredients, steps), every screen has the same basic building blocks. Let's learn what those are.

The 7 Things You'll See on Every Screen

No matter what device you're using or what you've opened, you'll see some combination of these 7 building blocks. Every website, every app, every screen — they're all just different arrangements of these same pieces:

1. Buttons

Buttons are the things you click or tap to make something happen. They usually look like colored rectangles with words inside them. When you see words like "Sign In," "Submit," "Next," "OK," or "Cancel" inside a colored rectangle — that's a button.

Here's what real buttons look like. You'll see these everywhere:

Notice how some buttons are blue, some green, some red? The color often hints at what the button does. Blue usually means "go ahead," green means "confirm/success," and red means "stop/delete/warning." Gray means "cancel" or "go back." This isn't a strict rule, but it's a very common pattern.

2. Text Fields (Input Boxes)

These are the boxes where you type information. Anytime a screen wants you to enter something — your name, your email address, a password, a search — it gives you a text field. They typically look like white rectangles with a thin border, and they often have faint gray "placeholder" text inside telling you what to type.

Email Address
Password

The faint text (like "yourname@example.com") is just a hint — it disappears as soon as you start typing. To use a text field: click on it once (you'll see a blinking line appear — that means it's ready for your typing), then start typing.

3. Links

Links are words that take you to another page when you click or tap them. They're like cross-references in a book — "see page 42" — except they take you there instantly. Links are usually blue and underlined, though sometimes they're other colors. The important clue: when you move your mouse pointer over a link on a computer, the little arrow changes into a pointing hand.

4. Menus

Menus are lists of choices. They work just like the table of contents in a book, or the menu at a restaurant — they show you your options. Some menus are always visible on the screen (especially at the top of websites), while others are hidden until you click on them. The hidden ones are sometimes called "dropdown menus" because they drop down when you click.

You'll often see three little horizontal lines ( ☰ ) — this icon is sometimes called the "hamburger menu" (because it looks a bit like a hamburger bun). Tapping or clicking those lines reveals a hidden menu.

5. Icons

Icons are small pictures that represent something. They're like the symbols on road signs — a red octagon means "stop" even without the word. Once you learn a few common icons, you'll see them everywhere:

6. Tabs

Tabs are like the labeled dividers in a filing cabinet or a binder. They let you switch between different sections without leaving the page. You'll see tabs at the top of your web browser (so you can have multiple websites open at once) and inside many apps and websites.

7. Pop-ups (Dialog Boxes)

These are small windows that appear on top of what you're looking at. They might ask you a question ("Are you sure you want to delete this?"), show you a message, or ask you to accept cookies. They usually have a button to close them — look for an "X" in the corner, or a button that says "OK," "Accept," or "Close."

Pop-ups can feel intrusive, but most are harmless. We'll talk about which ones to worry about in Module 6.

The big idea

Every website, every app, and every screen is just a different arrangement of these same 7 building blocks. When you see a new screen for the first time, take a breath and look for the buttons, text fields, links, menus, icons, and tabs. Once you spot them, you'll know exactly what that page is asking you to do. It's like walking into any kitchen — the fridge, stove, and sink are always there, just in different places.

Clicking, Tapping, and Typing: How You Talk to Your Devices

Your devices are waiting for you to tell them what to do. You "talk" to them through a few simple actions. These actions are slightly different on a computer vs. your phone/tablet:

👉
Practice right here! See the two labels below — "On Your Computer" and "On iPhone/iPad"? Those are tabs (one of the building blocks we just learned!). Try clicking the one that says "On iPhone/iPad" to switch to the other section. Then click "On Your Computer" to switch back.
This guide uses tabs, quizzes, and other interactive elements throughout — we'll always point them out so you know where to click.

Single Click (left mouse button) — This is the most common action. One quick press of the left mouse button. Use this to: select something, press a button, click a link, or put your cursor in a text field so you can type.

Double Click (left mouse button, quickly twice) — Two quick clicks in a row. Use this to: open a program or file on your desktop. It's like knocking twice on a door to open it.

Right Click (right mouse button) — One click of the right button. This opens a small menu with extra options — like a "secret menu." You won't need this often as a beginner, but it's good to know it exists.

Scrolling (mouse wheel or touchpad) — Roll the wheel on your mouse, or slide two fingers up/down on a laptop touchpad. This scrolls the page up and down — just like turning the pages of a book.

Typing — Click on a text field first (to make it active — you'll see a blinking vertical line), then type on your keyboard.

Tap — Touch the screen once, quickly and lightly, with one finger. This is the same as a click on a computer. Use it to press buttons, open apps, and select things.

Swipe — Touch the screen and slide your finger in a direction without lifting it. Swipe up/down to scroll through a page. Swipe left/right to move between pages or delete items.

Tap and Hold (Long Press) — Touch something and keep your finger on it for a second or two. This usually opens extra options — like right-clicking on a computer.

Pinch — Touch the screen with two fingers and bring them together (to zoom out / make things smaller) or spread them apart (to zoom in / make things bigger). Great for making text bigger on a webpage!

Typing — Tap on a text field and a keyboard will appear at the bottom of your screen. Tap the letters to type. When you're done, look for a button that says "Done," "Go," "Search," or "Return" on the keyboard.

The most important thing to remember

You cannot break anything by clicking or tapping the wrong thing. If you accidentally go to the wrong page, just press the Back button (the left arrow ← at the top of your browser, or swipe right from the left edge on your iPhone). If something strange happens, just close the window or app and start over. Exploring is how you learn!

Files & Folders: Your Digital Filing Cabinet

This is one of the most important concepts in all of computing. But the good news is — you already understand it, because it works exactly like a real-world filing system.

The filing cabinet analogy

Imagine the big filing cabinet in your home. It has drawers (on your computer, these are the main locations like "Documents," "Pictures," "Downloads"). Inside each drawer, you have folders with labels on them ("Tax Returns," "Medical Records," "Recipes"). Inside each folder, you have individual papers — these are your files (a PDF of your tax return, a photo, a letter).

On your computer, folders can go inside other folders — just like you might have a big folder labeled "Medical" with smaller folders inside for each family member. This is called "nesting," and it's how you keep things organized.

What About Those Extra Folders? (OneDrive & Shared Folders)

When you open File Explorer, you might notice some folders in the left sidebar that don't seem familiar — they might have someone else's name on them (like "Robert" or "rbear") or say something like "OneDrive" with a cloud icon next to them.

Here's what's happening: your computer is connected to a Microsoft 365 Family plan, which is a shared subscription. As part of that setup, some of Dad's cloud storage folders may show up alongside your own. Think of it like a shared filing cabinet where you can see the labels on his drawers, even though you have your own drawers.

What to do when you see unfamiliar folder names

Ignore any folders with Dad's name (like "Robert," "rbear," or anything you don't recognize). Those are his files stored in the cloud — they're not yours, and you don't need them.

Stick to your own familiar locations: When you need to find your files, click Desktop, Documents, Downloads, or Pictures in the left sidebar. These are YOUR folders. If you ever get confused about which folder is yours, look for the ones that don't have another person's name on them.

Making everything bigger and easier to read

Settings → System → Display → look for "Scale" — change it from 100% to 125% or 150%. This makes everything on screen larger: text, buttons, icons, everything. If 125% isn't big enough, try 150%.

You can also make just the text bigger: Settings → Accessibility → Text size, and slide the bar to the right.

Connecting to Wi-Fi (your internet connection)

Settings → Network & Internet → Wi-Fi. Make sure Wi-Fi is turned on (the switch should be blue). Your home network should connect automatically. If it's not connected, click the name of your network and enter the password.

The Wi-Fi password might be on a sticker on the back or bottom of your router — the small box with blinking lights, usually near your TV or desk.

Adjusting the volume

The quickest way: click the speaker icon in the bottom-right corner of your screen (in the Taskbar tray area). A slider appears — drag it left to lower the volume, right to raise it. You can also click the speaker icon on the left of the slider to mute/unmute.

Keeping your computer up to date

Settings → Windows Update. Your computer regularly needs to install updates to stay secure and running well. Usually this happens automatically, but sometimes it needs you to click "Restart now" to finish installing. When you see a message about updates, save any work you have open, then let it restart. It might take a few minutes.

The #1 trick on your computer: SEARCH

If you ever need to find anything — a setting, a program, a file — just click the magnifying glass 🔍 on the Taskbar and type what you're looking for. Type "Wi-Fi" and it shows you Wi-Fi settings. Type "brightness" and it takes you to brightness controls. Type "Chrome" and it opens Google Chrome. This one trick replaces the need to memorize where anything is. When in doubt, search!

What Is a Web Browser?

A web browser is the program you use to visit websites. On your computer, it's most likely Google Chrome (the colorful circle icon) or Microsoft Edge (the blue-green swirl icon). On your iPhone/iPad, the built-in browser is called Safari (the compass icon).

Think of it this way

The internet is like a giant city full of buildings. Each building is a website. Your web browser is the car you use to drive around this city. The address bar at the top of the browser is like a GPS — you type where you want to go, and it takes you there.

Chrome vs. Edge: Why It Matters Which Browser You Use

Your computer actually has two different web browsers installed on it. Think of them as two different cars parked in your driveway — they both drive to the same places, but your keys, mirrors, and seat settings are only saved in one of them.

Google Chrome (the colorful circle icon) is the browser where all your saved passwords, bookmarks, and settings live. This is the one you should always use. Microsoft Edge (the blue-green swirl) came pre-installed on your computer — it works fine, but your saved information isn't there, so websites won't remember you and you'd have to re-enter all your passwords.

What to do if the "wrong" browser opens

If you click a link (in an email, a document, or anywhere) and the website opens in Edge instead of Chrome, here's how to tell: look at the icon in the Taskbar at the bottom of your screen. If it's a blue-green swirl, you're in Edge. If it's a colorful circle, you're in Chrome.

Quick fix: Copy the web address from the Edge address bar (click on the address bar, press Ctrl+A to select it all, then Ctrl+C to copy). Then open Chrome (click the colorful circle icon on your Taskbar or Desktop), click the Chrome address bar, and press Ctrl+V to paste the address, then press Enter. Now you're on the same page but in your proper browser.

This has already been fixed on your computer so that links open in Chrome by default, but if it ever happens again, now you know what to do!

What are bookmarks and saved passwords?

Bookmarks are websites you've saved for quick access — like dog-earing a page in a book. They usually appear as a row of links near the top of your browser, just below the address bar. If you visit a website often (like AOL Mail or your bank), it's probably bookmarked in Chrome already.

Saved passwords means Chrome remembers your passwords for you. When you go to a sign-in page (like AOL Mail), Chrome fills in your username and password automatically so you don't have to type it every time. This only works in Chrome — it's another reason to always use Chrome and not Edge.

The internet is mostly a helpful, wonderful place. But just like in the real world, a few simple safety habits go a long way. And when things go wrong (they will — it happens to everyone!), there are easy fixes.

Passwords: Your Digital Keys

A password is like a key to your house. Every online account (bank, email, healthcare portal) has its own door, and you need a separate key for each one.

The three golden rules of passwords

Rule 1: Use a different password for each important account — especially your bank and email. If a bad person gets one key, they shouldn't be able to open all your doors.

Rule 2: Make passwords long and memorable — A great trick is to use a phrase you'll remember: MyGardenHas12Roses! or ILoveSundaysAndCoffee or BlueSkyInPhoenix2026. Long phrases are much harder for bad actors to crack than short passwords like "Celine1947."

Rule 3: Write them down in a safe, physical place — Keep a small notebook or a piece of paper with all your accounts, usernames, and passwords in a secure place at home (like a locked drawer). This is much better than using the same easy password everywhere or forgetting your passwords constantly.

What to write down for each account

For each account, note: (1) The website name and address (like "Bank of America - www.bankofamerica.com"), (2) Your username or email, (3) Your password, (4) Any security questions and answers. Keep this notebook in a safe place — not next to your computer where visitors could see it.

What to do if you forget a password

Don't panic! Every sign-in page has a link that says "Forgot Password?" or "Forgot your password?" Click it, and the website will send a password reset link to your email address. Open that email, click the link, and choose a new password. This is completely normal and safe — everyone does it!

Recognizing Scams & Phishing

Some people try to trick you into giving them your personal information or money. These tricks are called scams or phishing (pronounced "fishing" — because they're "fishing" for your information).

Red flags — if you see any of these, it's probably a scam

⚠️ "URGENT! Your account will be CLOSED in 24 hours!" — Real companies don't threaten you with scary deadlines in emails.

⚠️ "Click here to verify your password / account number" — No real company will ever ask for your password by email.

⚠️ The email address looks weird — It says it's from your bank, but the sender's address is something like "support@bank-secure-login-verify.xyz" instead of a normal address.

⚠️ "You've won $1,000,000!" — If you didn't enter a contest, you didn't win.

⚠️ Lots of spelling and grammar mistakes — Real companies proofread their emails.

The golden rule of online safety

When in doubt, don't click. If you receive a suspicious email claiming to be from your bank (or the IRS, or Amazon, or anyone), don't click any links in that email. Instead, open your web browser and type the real website address yourself, or call the company using a phone number you know is real (like the one on the back of your credit card). This way, you know for certain you're dealing with the real company.

Pop-ups & Cookie Messages

You'll encounter lots of pop-up messages while browsing. Most are harmless. Here's how to handle them:

✔
"Accept cookies?" — This is fine. Nearly every website shows this. Just click "Accept" or "OK" and move on. "Cookies" are how websites remember you.
✔
"Allow notifications?" — Almost always click "Don't Allow" or "Block." You don't want random websites sending you alerts.
✕
"Your computer has a virus! Call this number!" — This is ALWAYS a scam. Close the tab immediately. Your computer is fine. Real security warnings come from your antivirus software, not from websites.
✕
"Download this to speed up your PC!" — Never click this. It's unwanted software or a scam.
Closing stubborn pop-ups

If a pop-up won't go away: (1) Look for the X in its corner. (2) Try pressing the Escape (Esc) key on your keyboard (top-left corner). (3) If nothing works, close the entire browser tab by clicking the X on the tab.

Celine's Troubleshooting Toolkit

Things don't always work perfectly. This is completely normal — it happens to everyone, even tech experts! The good news is that a small number of simple fixes solve the vast majority of problems. Here is your go-to checklist:

When Something Isn't Working — Try These In Order
1
Refresh the page. If a website looks broken or won't load, click the refresh button (↻) in your browser. This reloads the page fresh. Fixes the problem more often than you'd think!
2
Close and reopen the app or program. If an app on your phone is acting up, swipe up to go home, then open it again. On a computer, close the window (click X) and reopen the program.
3
Restart the device. Turn your computer, phone, or tablet completely off, wait 10 seconds, and turn it back on. This genuinely fixes an enormous number of problems. It's the #1 thing IT professionals do!
4
Check your internet connection. If websites won't load, make sure you're connected to Wi-Fi. On your phone, check for the Wi-Fi icon at the top. On your computer, look in the bottom-right corner. If disconnected, try turning Wi-Fi off and back on in Settings.
5
Google the problem. Type your problem as a question: "AOL Mail not loading" or "iPhone won't connect to Wi-Fi." Millions of people have had the same problem, and the answer is usually in the first few results.
Think of it this way

When a lamp doesn't turn on, you check: Is it plugged in? Is the bulb burned out? Is the outlet working? Tech troubleshooting is exactly the same — start with the simplest explanation and work your way up. You don't need to understand electricity to change a lightbulb!

When Your Device Freezes or Stops Responding

Sometimes your computer, phone, or tablet just... stops. Nothing happens when you click or tap. The screen might be frozen, or an app might be stuck spinning. Don't panic — this happens to everyone and is almost always fixable.

If one program is frozen but the rest of the computer works:

Press Ctrl + Alt + Delete (hold all three keys at the same time — they're at the bottom-left and top-right of your keyboard). A screen appears — click "Task Manager." You'll see a list of running programs. Click the frozen one (it might say "Not Responding") and click "End Task." This closes just that program.

If the entire computer is frozen and nothing responds:

Press and hold the power button on your computer (the physical button you use to turn it on) for about 10 seconds until the screen goes dark. Wait 30 seconds, then press the power button once to turn it back on. You might lose any unsaved work, but the computer will be fine.

If an app is frozen on your iPhone/iPad:

Swipe up from the bottom and pause in the middle of the screen (don't swipe all the way up). This shows all your open apps as cards. Find the frozen app and swipe it up and off the top of the screen to close it. Then reopen it from the Home Screen.

If your entire iPhone/iPad is frozen:

Do a "force restart" — this is safe and doesn't erase anything: (1) Quickly press and release the Volume Up button. (2) Quickly press and release the Volume Down button. (3) Press and hold the Side button (the power button on the right side) until you see the Apple logo appear. Then let go and wait for it to restart.

Save your work often

If you're typing something important (a letter, an email, filling out a form), it's a good habit to save frequently. On the computer, press Ctrl+S (hold Ctrl, press S) to save your work. This way, if something freezes, you won't lose much. In a web browser, your typing in forms is usually NOT saved automatically — so if you're filling out a long form online, consider typing your answers in the Notes app first, then copying them over.

Running Out of Storage

Sometimes your phone or computer tells you that storage is almost full. This means the device is running out of room to save new things — like a filing cabinet that's stuffed to the brim.

Check what's using space: Settings → General → iPhone Storage. You'll see a colorful bar showing what's taking up room (usually Photos, Apps, and Messages are the biggest).

Easy ways to free up space:

(1) Delete old photos and videos — Open the Photos app, find photos you don't need, tap "Select" (top-right), tap the ones to delete, then tap the trash icon. Don't forget to also delete them from "Recently Deleted" (in the Albums tab) to truly free the space.

(2) Delete apps you don't use — Press and hold an app icon on the Home Screen until a menu appears. Tap "Remove App" → "Delete App."

(3) Clear old text messages — Open Messages, swipe left on old conversations, and tap "Delete."

Check what's using space: Settings → System → Storage. Windows shows you what's taking up room.

Easy ways to free up space:

(1) Empty the Recycle Bin — Right-click the Recycle Bin icon on your desktop and choose "Empty Recycle Bin." Deleted files sit there until you empty it.

(2) Clear your Downloads folder — Open File Explorer → Downloads. Delete files you no longer need (old installers, duplicate documents, etc.).

(3) Run "Storage Sense" — Go to Settings → System → Storage and turn on "Storage Sense." This automatically cleans up temporary files.

How to Ask for Help (From People and From the Internet)

Even after going through this entire guide, there will be times when you get stuck. That's completely normal! Here's how to get help effectively:

Asking the Internet

Google is your most powerful tool. When something isn't working or you don't know how to do something, type your question exactly as you'd ask a person:

🔍 "How do I change the font size on my iPhone"

🔍 "AOL Mail won't load what do I do"

🔍 "How to print a PDF on Windows"

🔍 "Bank of America how to set up bill pay"

The results will often include step-by-step guides (look for articles from the company itself, or from helpful sites like "How-To Geek" or Apple's support pages). There are also YouTube videos that walk you through things visually — sometimes seeing someone else do it is the easiest way to learn.

Asking a Person

When you need to ask a family member, friend, or tech support for help, here's what to include so they can help you quickly:

1
What device you're using — "I'm on my Windows computer" or "I'm on my iPhone."
2
What you were trying to do — "I was trying to pay my electric bill on the utility website."
3
What happened instead — "The page went blank" or "I got an error message that said..." or "Nothing happens when I click the button."
4
What you've already tried — "I refreshed the page and restarted the computer but it still doesn't work."
Take a screenshot!

A screenshot is a picture of what's on your screen. It's the best way to show someone exactly what you're seeing.

On your computer: Press the Print Screen key (sometimes labeled "PrtScn" — it's near the top-right of your keyboard). Then open an email and press Ctrl+V to paste it into the email.

On your iPhone/iPad: Press the Side button and the Volume Up button at the same time, quickly. You'll see a flash and hear a shutter sound. The screenshot is saved in your Photos app, and you can share it by text or email.

Calling Tech Support

If you need to call a company for tech help (your internet provider, your bank, Apple, etc.), here are some tips:

1
Have your account information ready before you call — your account number, the email address on the account, and your device nearby.
2
Write down what the support person tells you — the steps they walk you through, any reference numbers, and their name if possible.
3
Don't be embarrassed to say "Can you repeat that?" or "Can you slow down?" — That's their job, and they're happy to help.
For Apple help specifically

If you're having trouble with your iPhone or iPad, you can call Apple Support directly at 1-800-275-2273. They're patient and helpful. You can also visit any Apple Store and talk to someone at the "Genius Bar" — they'll help you for free with any Apple device issues.

Remote Help: Letting Family See Your Screen

Here's something wonderful: your computer is set up so that family members can see your screen and help you remotely — even if they're in another city. This is called remote access, and it's like having someone sit right next to you and look at your screen, except they're doing it through the internet.

Think of it this way

Imagine you're on the phone with someone, and instead of trying to describe what you see on your screen ("I see a box that says something about an error..."), they can just look at your screen themselves and see exactly what you see. They can even move your mouse and click things for you to fix the problem. That's remote help.

You have a tool called Chrome Remote Desktop installed in your Google Chrome browser. Here's how to use it when you need help:

1
Call or text one of the kids and let them know you need help with your computer.
2
Open Google Chrome (the colorful circle icon — remember, your browser!). In the address bar at the top, type remotedesktop.google.com/support and press Enter. You can also find it by looking for the Chrome Remote Desktop icon if it's been added to your extensions (it looks like a small screen with an arrow).
3
Click "Generate Code" under the section that says "Share this screen." A 12-digit number will appear on your screen. This is a one-time access code — it's like a temporary key that expires after a few minutes.
4
Read the code to your family member over the phone. They'll type it into their computer to connect.
5
You'll see a message asking if you want to share your screen. Click "Share" to allow the connection. Now they can see everything on your screen and control your mouse to help you!
6
When you're done, click "Stop Sharing" at the bottom of your screen, or just close the Chrome tab. The connection ends immediately and the access code stops working.
This is safe and private

Each access code only works once and expires quickly. Nobody can connect to your computer without you generating a new code and giving it to them. Once you stop sharing, the connection is completely cut off. You're always in control.

When to use remote help

Remote help is perfect for situations like: a setting got changed and you don't know how to change it back, a program looks different than usual and you're confused, you're trying to do something new and want someone to walk you through it, or a pop-up appeared that you're not sure about. Instead of trying to describe the problem over the phone, just say "Can you look at my screen?" — it saves a lot of frustration for everyone!
